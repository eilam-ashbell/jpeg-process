01

Light Enters the Lens

When you press the shutter button, light reflected from the scene enters through the camera lens. The lens is a curved piece of glass that bends (refracts) incoming light rays, focusing them onto a single point — the focal point.

Think of the lens as a funnel for light. Millions of photons stream through, each carrying color information from whatever they bounced off. The lens collects all these scattered rays and directs them precisely onto the tiny sensor chip waiting behind it.

02

The Image Sensor & Bayer Mosaic

Behind the lens sits the image sensor — a grid of millions of tiny light-sensitive cells called photosites. Each photosite can only measure the brightness of light, not its color. So how does a camera capture color?

The answer is the Bayer filter — a mosaic of red, green, and blue color filters placed over the sensor. Each photosite sees only one color. Notice there are twice as many green filters as red or blue — that's because human eyes are most sensitive to green, so the camera dedicates extra resolution to it.

What does the sensor actually see?

Bayer mosaic (sensor)

After demosaicing — a clever algorithm that fills in the missing color values for each photosite — every pixel now has a full set of three numbers: Red, Green, and Blue. These three values, each ranging from 0 to 255, mix together to create the final color you see.

At this point, your photo is just a massive grid of numbers. Each pixel takes 3 bytes of storage (one per channel). For a typical 12-megapixel photo, that's over 36 million bytes — about 36 MB of raw data. JPEG's job is to shrink this dramatically without ruining the image.

03

RGB Color Channels

Every pixel in your photo is a blend of three colors: red, green, and blue. By separating these channels, you can see each color's individual contribution. Look at the green channel — it's the brightest because it carries most of the detail your eyes care about.

JPEG processes these channels, but not in RGB form. The first clever trick is converting to a different color space that's better suited for compression. But first, let's see what RGB separation looks like.

04

YCbCr Color Space

JPEG doesn't work with red, green, and blue directly. Instead, it converts your image into a different color system called YCbCr. Y captures the brightness (luminance) — essentially a black-and-white version of your photo. Cb and Cr capture the color information (chrominance) — the blue-difference and red-difference signals.

Why bother? Because your eyes are much more sensitive to brightness than to color. By separating these, JPEG can aggressively compress the color information (Cb, Cr) while keeping the brightness (Y) sharp — and you'll barely notice the difference.

Cb = −0.169 × R  −  0.331 × G  +  0.500 × B  +  128
Cr =  0.500 × R  −  0.419 × G  −  0.081 × B  +  128

Cb and Cr store only the difference from brightness. Their values cluster around 128 (neutral) — much less variation than Y. This is why JPEG can aggressively compress them in the next step.
05

Chroma Subsampling

Here's where compression actually begins. Since your eyes can't detect fine color detail, JPEG shrinks the Cb and Cr channels — keeping only some color samples and reconstructing the rest from neighbors. The brightness (Y) channel stays at full resolution.

The number scheme (4:4:4, 4:2:2, 4:2:0) describes how many Cb/Cr samples are kept per row of 4 luma pixels. 4:2:0 — the most common — cuts color data by 75%.

06

8×8 Block Division

JPEG doesn't compress the whole image at once. Instead, it divides your photo into a grid of tiny 8×8 pixel blocks — just 64 pixels each. For a 1080p image, that's over 32,000 blocks, each processed independently.

Why 8×8? It's a sweet spot — small enough to capture local patterns efficiently, but large enough to contain meaningful visual information. Every block goes through the same compression pipeline from here on. Click any block to follow its journey through the next steps.

07

Discrete Cosine Transform

Here's where the math magic happens. The Discrete Cosine Transform takes each 8×8 block of pixel values and converts them into 64 frequency values — think of it like a graphic equalizer for images. Low frequencies (top-left) capture smooth gradients, high frequencies (bottom-right) capture sharp edges and fine details.

The brilliant insight: most of the visual energy in a typical photo block concentrates in just a few low-frequency values. The high-frequency coefficients are often tiny or zero. This is why JPEG can throw away data without you noticing — it's discarding frequencies your eyes barely register.

08

Quantization

Quantization is where JPEG actually loses information — permanently. Each DCT coefficient is divided by a number from the quantization table, then rounded to the nearest integer. Large divisors for high-frequency coefficients crush small values to zero, while the important low-frequency values survive.

The quality slider you see on image editors controls exactly this step. Quality 100 means gentle quantization (large file, high quality). Quality 1 means aggressive quantization (tiny file, blocky mess). At quality 75 — a common default — about 70% of the coefficients become zero. That's a lot of data you no longer need to store.

09

Zigzag Scanning

The quantized 8×8 matrix still has two dimensions, but the compressed file is a one-dimensional stream of bytes. JPEG reads the matrix in a zigzag pattern — starting at the top-left corner and sweeping diagonally back and forth to the bottom-right.

Why zigzag? After quantization, the non-zero values cluster in the top-left (low frequencies) and zeros fill the bottom-right (high frequencies). The zigzag path puts all those zeros at the end of the sequence, making them easy to compress in the next step.

10

Run-Length Encoding

Now that all those zeros are clustered at the end of the sequence, JPEG uses a lossless trick called Run-Length Encoding. Instead of storing each zero individually, it counts consecutive zeros and writes a single pair: (number of zeros, next non-zero value). Runs of 15+ zeros are written as (15, 0).

The sequence ends with a special EOB (End of Block) marker that says "everything after this is zero." For a heavily quantized block, this marker might appear after just a handful of values — compressing 64 numbers down to maybe 10 or 15 tokens.

11

DPCM — DC Differencing

Before the final encoding step, JPEG applies one more lossless trick to the DC coefficient — the very first value in each block's RLE sequence, representing its average brightness. Instead of storing the raw DC value, JPEG stores only the difference from the previous block's DC. This technique is called Differential Pulse Code Modulation (DPCM).

Why does this help? Adjacent 8×8 blocks in an image tend to have very similar average brightness — so the differences between their DC values are usually tiny. Small numbers need fewer bits to represent, which means the next step (Huffman coding) can assign them very short codes. The very first block in the scan has no predecessor, so its DC value is encoded as-is.

Importantly, DPCM operates independently per channel — Y tracks its own previous DC, Cb tracks its own, and Cr tracks its own. This keeps the differencing clean and prevents cross-channel interference.

12

Huffman Coding

The last step is Huffman coding — a lossless technique that assigns variable-length binary codes to symbols. Frequent symbols get very short codes (maybe 2–3 bits), while rare ones get longer codes. It's the same principle that makes Morse code efficient — "E" is just a single dot.

But JPEG doesn't Huffman-encode the full (skip, value) token directly. Instead, it splits each token into two parts: a Huffman code for the symbol's category (how many bits the value needs), followed by the actual value in raw binary — not Huffman coded. This keeps the Huffman table small and fixed-size.

For DC coefficients, the Huffman table has just 12 entries — one per category (0–11). For AC coefficients, each (run-length, category) pair gets its own entry — about 162 possible symbols, though most images use far fewer. JPEG uses 4 separate Huffman tables: DC-luma, DC-chroma, AC-luma, and AC-chroma. Cb and Cr share the same chroma tables, but each channel is encoded independently with its own DC coefficient, its own AC tokens, and its own EOB marker.

The result is a stream of bits that's as compact as possible without losing any information. Combined with all the previous steps, your original 36 MB of raw pixel data is now compressed to around 300 KB — over 100 times smaller. That's the magic of JPEG.

13

Building the Bitstream

The Huffman codes are just strings of 0s and 1s — but a JPEG file is a sequence of bytes. The scan data lives in the SOS (Start of Scan) segment, which has a fixed 12-byte header followed by the raw packed bitstream. A JPEG file is structured as a series of marker segments, each beginning with 0xFF — and SOS is where all the compressed image data lives.

The scan data is not a flat, unstructured stream. Each 8×8 block is encoded with a strict two-part layout: the DC coefficient comes first — that's the top-left value from the quantized DCT block, representing the block's average brightness. Then the AC coefficients follow in zigzag frequency order, using run-length (skip, value) pairs ending in EOB. DC and AC use entirely separate Huffman tables, which is why the SOS header explicitly lists both a DC table ID and an AC table ID for each channel.

Remember: the DC value here is not the raw coefficient — it's the DPCM difference we computed earlier. That small delta gets a short Huffman code, which is exactly why the differencing step pays off.

Live JPEG Compression

Now let's see the complete JPEG pipeline applied to your entire photo. Drag the quality slider to watch the file size change — and notice the visual artifacts that appear at lower quality settings. Below quality 20, you'll start seeing obvious 8×8 block boundaries — that's the block-based processing showing through.

    Original vs Compressed

    Drag the divider to compare your original photo with the JPEG-compressed version. Pay attention to edges, gradients, and areas of solid color — those are where artifacts are most visible.

    The four main JPEG artifacts each have a name. Blockiness: visible 8×8 grid. Color banding: smooth gradients become stair-stepped. Ringing: halos around sharp edges. Mosquito noise: shimmering patterns near high-contrast boundaries.

    Undo JPEG: Decode Pipeline

    When your computer opens a JPEG file, it runs the entire pipeline in reverse. Let's watch each step reconstruct the image — and see exactly where the permanently lost information creates the differences you noticed in the comparison.

    The lossless steps (Huffman and RLE decoding) recover perfectly — no information was lost there. But dequantization can only multiply by the quantization table values; it can't recover the fractions that were rounded away. That permanent loss is why JPEG is called a "lossy" format.
